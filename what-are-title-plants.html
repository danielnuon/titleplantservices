ABOUT TITLE PLANTS

A title plant, sometimes called an abstract plant, is a collection of recorded documents that track the history of a real estate parcel. States require that these records go back either to the original land grant or a set number of years, typically 20-50 years. Each day, new data is added as recorded documents are indexed and placed into the plant.

Modern title plants are digital and virtual, stored on large-scale systems called Storage Area Networks (SANs), which house millions of documents. These SANs are redundant, meaning if one drive fails, others take over. The data is also backed up in the cloud for security. While original title plants were paper-based and later moved to film (such as 16mm film, microfilm, and aperture cards), modern plants are now digital.

The size of a title plant depends on the number of real estate parcels and transactions in a county. For example, smaller counties with fewer parcels may have fewer than a million documents, while larger counties like King County, WA, could have tens of millions.
